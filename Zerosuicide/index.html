Get Started

Understanding what is going on

Suicide is the act of taking one's own life or killing oneself. According to the World Health Organisation (WHO), there are more than 700,000 cases of suicide each year and many more cases of attempted suicide. Suicide, which day by day is becoming a major health problem in every country in every corner of the world has a long and impactful effect on people, families and friends. Suicide can occur any time in an individual's life and it was the fourth leading cause of death among 15-29 years-old globally in 2019.

In Malaysia, suicide is also an alarming issue that we need to tackle fast. According to Bukit Aman CID director Comm Datuk Seri Abd Jalil Hassan, there are a total of 631 cases of suicide in 2020 while 468 cases of suicide between January and May 2021. What's worse, most suicide cases and attempted suicide cases go unreported or unrecorded. Data, statistics and evaluation of such cases are also rarely done and left unattended.

On the right side is a graph that showcase the estimated suicide rate among Malaysians by WHO.

  • 01 Values of the estimated suicide rates

    The line on the graph showcase the estimated rates of suicides for both sexes (male and female) from 2000 until 2019 by WHO. The value is per 100k citizens. For instance, in 2009, the estimated value of suicide per 100k citizens stands at 4.60 which is a small increment from 2008 (4.50).

  • 02 Pattern and trend of the estimated rates

    If we look at the graph, instantly we can see a pattern on the estimated suicide rates published by WHO. A distinct pattern that we can highlight is that the number of times the rate increase is much more than the number off times the rate decrease. On top of that, after 2013, WHO estimated that the suicide rates increase tremendously and did not decrease even once in a span of 6 years.

  • 03 Why is it estimated suicide rate?

    It is the estimated suicide rate and not recorded suicide rate solely because through our research, there is no datasets that we can access relating to the suicide rates recorded in Malaysia. The data that is accessible to the public on suicide is close to none and that is an alarming issue.

PROJECTION

The second area is the projection we from SKADOOSH has made to showcase the possibility of what could happen if we ignore this important issue or what could happen if we decide to take the necessary action to ensure the suicide case we have in our country is correctly handled.




The graph on the left is the prediction on the estimated suicide rate in Malaysia. The projection is 10 years forward. As we can see, the estimated suicide rate drops steadily after 2019 but it started to increase after 2023. The lowest point (2023, with value 4.52) during the decrease in number of cases after 2019 still has a bigger value than the lowest point 2013 (4.40). It can be concluded that the estimated suicide rate would decrease but the lowest point will be bigger than the previous lowest point. On the other side, the graph on the right is the prediction on the number of suicide cases by self harm. The projection is also 10 years forward and the pattern is fairly the same. PROPOSITION

After careful observations, thoughtful researching, and numerous data analyzation and visualization, we from SKADOOSH has come up with several recommended solution to this issue we are facing as a nation. These solutions might not be the answer to all of the problems we have but we believe it is a step forward in providing help to those who needs it and to ensure that our nation is ready to face this uphill battle.

  • Provide guidelines on suicide prevention

    Ministry of Health can provide proper and suitable guidelines on suicide prevention to the people through the usage of chatbot. Chatbots can be easily accessible for everyone and having a suicide prevention guideline at the tip of your fingertips is handy and at the same time equip you with the right knowledge on how to handle scenarios relating to suicide.

  • Improve and create more platforms that work as helplines

    As of this point, most hotlines that provide help to suicidal people work individually. NGOs, independent clinics and even volunteers are not supported enough or do not receive a helping hand in handling this ginormous issue our country is facing. The government could centralise such organizations and individuals and inform the public of such efforts they are making and at the same time lending them a helping hand to ensure their effort is accessible to everyone so that we can combat suicide together.

  • Improving data quality

    Previous efforts such as the National Suicide Registry Malaysia (NSRM) should be reinstated to help gather more accurate and precise data to ensure our nation is equipped with the right and proper knowledge on what and how suicide is affecting the country. Department of Statistics Malaysia (DOSM) can also work closely with such organization to improve the data quality on suicide so we can come out with more in-depth and targeted solution.

  • Create awareness and more open space to talk about suicide

    The government can create more awareness campaign to remove this stigma that suicide is a taboo topic to talk about. With such effort, it will indirectly create more safe space for our citizen to seek help and talk about their thoughts and how they feel. Educating the citizens that it is fine to share what they feel is important so that the people who are effected knows that they are not alone in this fight.
